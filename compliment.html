The Compliment Hub

Introduction

The Compliment Hub is a multi-lingual compliment sending website used to research the psychology of giving and receiving compliments across different cultures. After sending or receiving a compliment, the user is presented with an optional short research survey.

I worked in a team to develop The Compliment Hub for a client and focused mostly on front-end development. The project was created with an HTML and Javascript frontend, and a Node.js and MySQL backend.

Our work in this project included :

  • Working closely with the client to match their vision
  • Interviewing users to understand their needs and expectations
  • Planning the project front-end and back-end architecture
  • Creating user flows and scenarios to generate a user-centered design
  • Developing and deploying the website
  • Performance and security testing

Working with the Client

Our client was a postdoctoral research fellow from University of Chicago looking to "investigate the psychology of giving and receiving compliments in different cultures."

Primary Research Questions

  1. Compliment and happiness: Do compliments make people happy across cultures?
  2. Undervaluing compliment: Do people across cultures underestimate the positive impact of compliments on their recipients? And overestimate how awkward/embarrassed/shy others will feel when receiving compliments?

Secondary Research Questions

  1. Frequency of compliment-giving behavior: Do people in all cultures perceive a deficit of compliment-giving (compared to how often you think you should…, how often do you actually…)? How often do people give compliments in different cultures? Is behavior frequency correlated with interpersonal miscalibration?
  2. What makes a good compliment in the eyes of the expressers and recipients?
  3. Taxonomy of compliments: What do people find praiseworthy in different cultures?

Our team met frequently with our client to give updates and refine our work. We worked closely with the client to match her vision of the website and best help her achieve her research goal.

User Interviews

We interviewed users to understand their needs and expectations about a compliment sending website. We asked the following questions to three users after creating a first design draft :

Interview Questions

  • What functionality do you expect from our project?
  • What features do you think would be most important out of the ones we have planned? (Email System, Survey, Card System Kindness Map, Multilinguality, Sharing on Social Media, Contact Us)
  • How intuitive are the current layouts/designs?

Response Summary

We found that all three users preferred clean and simple UI. One user wanted premade compliment templates. The Email System and Card System were thought to be most important, and the Contact Us and Sharing on Social Media were thought to be least important. There were mixed responses to our initial design. While all users thought it was intuitive and easy to use, one user thought the interface should be more minimalistic and one user noted the layout was inconsitent between pages.

Performance and Security Testing

After creating the website, we performed tests to evaluate usability and security.

Our project website, The Compliment Hub, is very usability and front-end focused, so we prioritized HTML/CSS validation, performance testing, and front-end testing. However, because we still used a backend and because we wanted to protect our database and users, we did backend and security testing as well.

HTML/CSS Validation

We used WC3 on many website pages, which uncovered that our website was missing many accessibility elements such as lang tags and text alternatives for images. We then added these elements to improve accessibility. We also discovered we had invalid tags, which we removed afterwards.

Performance Testing

We also ran our website’s homepage through GTmetrix, and we received a Pagespeed Score of 98% (A), as well as a YSlow Score of 96% (A). This was very positive, and it showed that our group had done a good job with performance speed.

Frontend Testing

We found the edge case of when the user jumps straight to submitting a compliment card without entering any information, it leads to our kindness map not loading. We broke down our code into smaller, more manageable functions and used gunit testing, and discovered that our problem was that we did not handle invalid or empty fields in our kindness map displaying algorithm. We added checks into our code, and also made it so the user cannot submit a compliment without entering at least the very basic information.

Backend Testing

We wrote a basic backend testing suite with Jasmine, and with it, discovered that our routing for the contact selectively worked; it did not work when going from any page other than the home page because it was a href on the homepage. This was an edge case, and we fixed this with a response.redirect to home.html. It also discovered that the algorithm on the backend for computing “kindness chains” returned true in the test case when it should have returned false, leading us to fix that bug as well.

Security Testing

To test this, we manually tried some common attacks such as JS injections, SQL injections, and Cross-Site scripting. We discovered that we were vulnerable to injection attacks, so we made our application more robust by validating user inputs on the server side before executing functions requiring those inputs. This protects it from most injection attacks as opposed to more specific threats.

Takeaways

I learned a lot throughout this project about interacting with a client, designing for usability, website code development, and security.
